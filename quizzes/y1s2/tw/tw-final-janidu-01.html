Passage 1: Digital Libraries in Higher Education

Digital libraries have become an important part of university education. Unlike traditional libraries, which mainly depend on printed books and physical visits, digital libraries allow students to access academic materials through computers and mobile devices. These materials may include e-books, journal articles, lecture notes, past papers, research reports and multimedia resources.

One major advantage of a digital library is accessibility. Students can use resources at any time of the day, even when the physical library is closed. This is especially helpful for students who live far from campus or who study while working part-time. Search tools also make it easier to find information quickly. Instead of looking through shelves, students can search by author, title, keyword or subject.

However, digital libraries do not automatically improve learning. Students must know how to evaluate the quality of online sources. Some materials found online may be outdated, biased or unsuitable for academic work. Therefore, universities often provide training on database searching, citation tools and plagiarism avoidance.

Another concern is unequal access. A digital library requires devices, internet connectivity and basic digital skills. Students from low-income backgrounds may face difficulties if they do not have reliable internet at home. As a result, universities should provide computer labs, Wi-Fi facilities and technical support.

Overall, digital libraries can improve academic learning when they are combined with proper training and fair access. They are not a replacement for academic judgement, but they are a powerful support system for modern students.

Passage 2: The Role of Feedback in Academic Writing

A. Academic writing improves through practice, revision and feedback. Many students believe that writing ends after the first draft, but experienced writers understand that a first draft is only a starting point. Feedback helps writers identify unclear ideas, weak evidence, grammar problems and issues in structure.

B. Lecturer feedback is valuable because it is usually based on assessment criteria. A lecturer can explain whether the answer addresses the question, whether the paragraph has a clear topic sentence and whether the evidence supports the argument. However, lecturers may not have enough time to correct every sentence in detail, especially in large classes.

C. Peer feedback can support this process. When students read each other’s work, they become more aware of audience expectations. If a classmate cannot understand an explanation, the writer may need to add examples or change the order of ideas. Giving feedback also helps the reviewer notice similar weaknesses in the reviewer’s own writing.

D. Feedback is useful only when it is specific and respectful. Comments such as “good” or “bad” do not tell the writer what should be improved. A useful comment might say, “The topic sentence is clear, but the second example does not directly support the main point.” Such feedback gives the writer a clear direction for revision.

E. Finally, students should not treat feedback as criticism of their ability. Feedback is part of the writing process. A strong academic writer uses feedback to make informed changes and produce a clearer final answer.

Passage 3: Reducing Plastic Use on Campus

Many universities have introduced campaigns to reduce single-use plastic on campus. Single-use plastic items, such as water bottles, straws, food containers and shopping bags, are used briefly but may remain in the environment for many years. When these items are not disposed of properly, they can block drains, pollute waterways and harm animals.

Campus plastic-reduction programmes usually begin with awareness. Posters, workshops and social media messages can explain the environmental impact of plastic waste. However, awareness alone is not enough. Students and staff need practical alternatives, such as refillable water stations, reusable food containers and cloth bags.

Some universities also introduce rules to reduce plastic. For example, cafeterias may stop selling drinks in disposable bottles or charge extra for single-use packaging. These rules can encourage people to change habits, but they should be introduced gradually so that the campus community has time to adjust.

The success of a plastic-reduction campaign depends on cooperation among students, staff, vendors and administrators. If convenient alternatives are available and the reasons for change are clearly communicated, the campus can significantly reduce plastic waste.
